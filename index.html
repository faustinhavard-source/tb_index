How to find your idea

The purpose of this doc is to give you prompts that can help you find your startup idea. Before we start, there are two important things to know about this topic.

  1. When you're looking for your startup idea, our advice is to start from what you uniquely know, have seen, or can build. You're not here to just build any company, you want to build the company you are most likely to win with, and you're most likely to actually enjoy building.
  2. Another thing to understand about a startup idea, is that it is a starting point, not a destination. You will test it, break it, reshape it, and eventually arrive at something you could not never have planned from day one. Ideation is not finished until you reach PMF. Everything before that is iteration.

Understand what your edge is

Most early founders approach startups with a goal-first mindset: they try to decide what the best idea is, and then figure out how to build it. But under real uncertainty, which is what founding is, the winning pattern is usually the opposite: start from the means that you actually have, and discover what outcomes become possible.

That's why a helpful starting point is what we call your edge, aka your unfair advantage in solving a problem compared to other founders. Your edge is built on:

  • Who you are: your skills, taste, personality, obsessions
  • What you know: your domains, insights, experiences, the things you understand at a level that few others do
  • Who you know: your network, access, the people who will take your call

To help you actually understand your edges, describe the key few things you can do that most founders cannot. Name specifics that differentiate you from the crowd and be careful not to be generic. Eg, a terrible example of an edge is: I worked at company X.

Here are some better examples:

  • I designed the guardrail architecture that let a healthcare company deploy LLMs into clinical note summarization
  • I built the perception-to-control stack that let a warehouse robots grasp thousands of SKU variants
  • I spent my PhD and postdoc developing spectral identification methods for bacterial detection so I know exactly which wavelength signatures differentiate pathogenic strains
  • I can get 20 procurement-grade conversations in fintech risk within a week
  • I know exactly how this buyer really buys
  • I built the self-serve growth loop that took a design tool from 50K to 2M MAU in 12 months

Prompts to help you find ideas

Find a belief about the future, and build towards that

Based on what you and your cofounder uniquely know and can build, what do you believe could be fundamentally different in 5-10 years? Make sure that you can name both the belief about what will be different in the future, but also what the cause / mechanism is that will lead to this change. Naming a mechanism is the only way to add credibility to your belief.

  • What do technologies available now, or in the imminent future, allow us to build that will fundamentally disrupt a sector/category or create an entirely new category? How and why?
  • What are your most extreme, counter-intuitive or non-trivial beliefs in that area?

Then, think about how your team's belief would be brought to reality. Specifically, come up with a hunch on how you might solve the problem for your customer, aka what product you might create to bring your belief to life.

Canva
Founded 2012. $4Bn ARR, 265 million users, $42Bn+ valuation.

Melanie Perkins was a 19-year-old university student in Perth, Australia, teaching classmates how to use Photoshop and InDesign. She watched them spend an entire semester just learning where the buttons were. Her mother, a teacher, had spent hundreds of hours wrestling with InDesign to create a single school publication. The software existed, but it was built for professional designers, not for the millions of people who needed to make something look decent and had no idea how. That gap between how hard design was and how easy it should be was Perkins' edge: not a technical edge, but a deep understanding of the non-designer's struggle with design tools.

Another edge: she had access to one specific use case where the problem was acute, high school yearbooks. Teachers with no design training were expected to produce beautiful publications, and their tools were either overwhelmingly complex or laughably primitive.

Perkins' core belief about the future was that visual communication would overtake text as the primary language of the internet. She saw that everyone, not just designers, would need to create and share visual content online. Although it may seem obvious today that image would become central to online communication, it wasn't in 2012. The prevailing mental model of the internet was still an online newspaper or magazine at best: text-heavy, link-driven, designed to be read. The idea that a drag-and-drop design tool for non-designers could become one of the most-used software products in the world would have sounded naive (and it did to the first 100+ investors that passed on Canva).

The enabling forces behind this belief were: browser technology was maturing (HTML5 was finally making rich in-browser editing viable), social media was exploding (Facebook, Instagram had hit 50 million in under two years, Pinterest was the fastest-growing site in history), and the shift to mobile meant every piece of content was now competing for attention on a small screen where images dominated and text walls didn't perform. This meant that small businesses and individuals who had never needed a designer before were suddenly expected to produce visual content daily: social posts, ads, headers, etc. Their options were either $600/year Adobe subscriptions or Microsoft Paint.

Canva was founded in 2012. Today, Canva has $4Bn ARR, 265 million users, and a valuation of over $42Bn.

This is hard, it shouldn't be this hard

Another prompt : notice a process that you know well, and that is absurdly slow, expensive, or complex. Try to figure out: why is this so hard?

When a process feels absurdly difficult, that often points to a structural misalignment: the tools, systems, or institutions that serve a need were built for a different era, a different technology stack, or a different set of user expectations.

Be very wary here, though. This framework is the one most prone to generate tarpit ideas. Many daily consumer problems are shared by everyone, which usually yields obvious, crowded ideas (and thin differentiation). This mainly works if we're talking about expert frustration on a specific process, not consumer annoyance. Eg, please avoid the classic: "why is it so hard to find that one document buried somewhere between Notion, Drive, and Slack".

This prompt works best if you can think of a pain that:

  • happens inside a domain where you have an edge
  • maps to a real budget or real risk (money, time, compliance, revenue loss)
  • hides structural change (cost curve collapse, regulatory shift, distribution shift) that makes rebuilding the workflow newly possible. This is hard ideas often start as a product problem but become a system design problem (distribution, incentives, cost).
Figma
Founded 2011. $1Bn+ ARR, 13 million users, IPO at ~$20Bn.

In 2012, design was still a single-player, offline experience. Designers used Sketch (Mac-only) or Adobe's desktop tools, and when they wanted feedback, they exported PNGs, emailed them around, and collected comments in separate tools: a horrifying and manual process. Version control didn't exist. Every handoff between designer and engineer involved friction, duplication, and lost context. It shouldn't have been that hard.

Dylan Field had suffered through this during an internship at a company called Flipboard. When he was a CS student at Brown, his teaching assistant and friend Evan Wallace showed him a WebGL demo. Most people who saw this thought about the unlock for gaming. But Dylan saw something else : he realized that browser technology had quietly matured to the point where you could build serious, high-performance graphics applications entirely in the browser. That's what unlocked the idea for figma. Before this tech unlock, the horrifying and manual design process would have remained just as hard, despite tons of people suffering from it.

Figma was founded in 2011. Today, Figma has $1Bn+ ARR, 13 million users, and IPOed at valuation of approx $20Bn.

Spore.Bio
Founded 2023 out of EF. Raised over €30M including a €22M Series A.

Amine Raji, CEO of SporeBio, spent six years as a manufacturing engineer at Nestlé. There, he saw how every step of food production had been hyper-optimized: supply chains timed to the minute, lean manufacturing everywhere, every process calibrated for speed (and thus, profit). And then, in the middle of all this optimization, there was a five-day wall.

A critical step, which was checking whether a product was contaminated with bacteria, still relied on growing cultures in Petri dishes, a technique that hadn't fundamentally changed in over a century. You literally had to wait for bacteria to grow, which meant 5 days in food and beverage, and up to 14 days in cosmetics.

Amine left Nestlé, joined Entrepreneurs First in Paris, and met Maxime Mistretta, a PhD graduate and postdoc from the Pasteur Institute specializing in microbiology. Their combination was textbook Edge + Edge: Raji had the market edge (he knew the factory floor, the procurement process, the buyer), and Mistretta had the technical edge (he could build a completely different detection system).

Their approach sidesteps traditional molecular biology entirely: instead of growing bacteria and waiting, Spore.Bio fires light at specific wavelengths toward a sample and uses a pre-trained deep learning model to identify contamination from the spectral signature, in under ten minutes. Advances in deep learning and spectral analysis made it newly possible to identify bacterial contamination without culture-based methods, a capability that simply did not exist years earlier.

Spore.Bio was founded in 2023 out of Entrepreneurs First. They have raised over €30 million to date, including a €22 million Series A led by Singular in early 2025.

Ride the wave, but look for what's missing

Major technological shifts create entire new categories of companies, and that's often where the biggest companies in the world are created. The internet created Google, Facebook, and Amazon. Mobile created Uber, Instagram, and WhatsApp. Cloud infrastructure created Snowflake and Datadog. AI is creating the next wave right now.

Start with a platform shift statement you are excited about and willing to defend. Then:

  • Describe who gains new power (developer, consumer, operator) and what new behavior becomes normal.
  • List the second-order problems that must be solved for the shift to fully realize (trust, coordination, workflow integration, compliance, cost control).
  • Pick one missing layer that, if solved, becomes a control point, aka the place workflow lives, the interface everyone uses, or the infrastructure everyone routes through.

A word of caution: today, many founders are actually just building a wrapper. The framework doesn't work well if you are just using a new technology, aka "let's put an LLM in front of an existing workflow". The companies in the examples below didn't just use a new technology. They identified a structural gap that the technology created, and they became the answer to that gap.

Cursor
Founded 2022. $2Bn+ revenue, 1M+ DAUs, $30Bn+ valuation.

By 2022, LLMs could generate, understand, and reason about code at a high level. A new behavior was becoming normal: developers interacting with AI as a collaborator, not just a linter. GitHub Copilot proved this was real: within a year of launch, it was generating roughly 40% of the code written by developers. But the second-order problems were everywhere. Copilot was built as an extension, a plugin bolted on top of existing editors. It could autocomplete lines, but it couldn't understand an entire codebase, couldn't coordinate changes across multiple files, and couldn't reason about the project as a whole. The architecture was wrong: the missing layer was not "better autocomplete," it was a development environment rebuilt from the ground up with AI at the core.

Four MIT computer science students, Michael Truell, Sualeh Asif, Arvid Lunnemark, and Aman Sanger, saw this gap. After a year of trying, ideating, an pivoting, they came up with the beginning of Cursor as we know it today. Originally, they spent nearly a year trying to build AI autocomplete for mechanical engineering CAD tools, before realizing they had terrible founder-market fit: they weren't mechanical engineers, didn't understand the workflows, and the training data wasn't there the way it was for code.

Moving back to standard software dev, they forked VS Code and rebuilt it as an AI-native editor. The decision to fork, rather than build another plugin, was the critical architectural bet: by owning the entire editor surface, they could embed AI into every layer, from code completion to multi-file editing to codebase-wide understanding.

Cursor was founded in 2022. It went from launch to $1 billion in ARR faster than any B2B software company in history. Today, Cursor has $2Bn+ revenue, 1M+ DAUs, and is valued at over $30Bn.

Datadog
Founded 2010. $3.5Bn+ revenue, $42Bn market cap.

In 2010, cloud computing was just beginning to move from experiment to production infrastructure. AWS had launched its core compute service only four years earlier. The platform shift was clear: companies were moving their entire infra to the cloud. Engineering teams were now spinning up and shutting down servers constantly, on demand, instead of managing fixed fixed, on-prem servers.

This shift created a massive second-order problem. All the existing monitoring tools had been built for a world of permanent, physical servers. In the cloud, servers appear and disappear constantly. You can't monitor what you can't see, and the environment was now changing faster than humans could track. That was the missing layer: the cloud had given companies a fundamentally new way to run infrastructure, but no one had built the visibility layer that this new infrastructure required to actually work.

Olivier Pomel and Alexis Lê-Quôc saw this gap. They started Datadog in New York in 2010, and for the first six months, spoke to engineering teams migrating to the cloud, to understand exactly what the pain looked like. They ended up building the monitoring system that only made sense in a cloud world, and by doing so, they became the infrastructure layer that every cloud-adopting company eventually routed through.

Datadog was founded in 2010. Today, Datadog has $3.5Bn+ revenue and a $42Bn market cap.

Unbundle and rebundle

Most markets are the result of years of accumulation: tools added on top of tools, workflows stitched together, people compensating for gaps with manual work. Over time, this creates either bloated bundles (one tool trying to do everything badly) or fragmented workflows (ten tools loosely connected, with constant friction between them).

Unbundling happens when a startup takes a specific piece of a large product and does it 10x better. These companies succeeded because they identified a specific function that was trapped inside a bloated bundle, and they liberated and 10x improved it.

Rebundling is more powerful, and less intuitive. It happens when a startup looks at a fragmented system and says: this should not be ten steps, ten tools, and ten people. It should be one system. The winner is the one that redefines where the workflow lives.

The reason unbundling or rebundling works is always structural. Something changed (the cost of building, distribution, etc). Without that structural change, even if existing systems may be inefficient, they will usually persist because the economics haven't shifted.

Rippling
Founded 2017. Over 10,000 customers, $570M+ ARR, valued at $16.8Bn+.

When Parker Conrad started Rippling, the HR, IT, and finance software landscape was the textbook case of fragmentation. A company onboarding a new employee had to enter the same information into a payroll system, a benefits platform, an IT device management tool, an app provisioning system, an expense tracker, and half a dozen others. Each tool had its own login, its own data model, its own quirks. If an employee changed roles, went on leave, or left the company, someone had to manually update every system.

This fragmentation persisted because, for a decade, the startup playbook said to focus on one narrow thing. Every SaaS company followed the unbundling logic: take one slice of the workflow, build a solution, and do it extremely well. The result was a landscape of dozens of excellent, narrow tools that didn't talk to each other.

What made rebundling newly possible and necessary was that the SaaS era had overshot. The same wave that created all these solutions also created the problem: companies were now managing 100+ separate software tools, each holding a different slice of the same employee data. The cost of fragmentation was starting to become real compliance risk (especially as companies hired across countries and jurisdictions), security gaps when offboarding was delayed across systems, and a growing tax on every HR and IT team's time that got worse with every new tool added.

Conrad's founding insight was architectural: the real product wasn't any single module but the unified employee data layer underneath all of them. If you built a single system of record for every piece of information about every employee, then every module built on top of it could share data, automate handoffs, and eliminate the friction that fragmentation created.

Rippling was founded in 2017. Today, Rippling has over 10,000 customers, $570M+ ARR, and is valued at $16.8Bn+.

Think about which tools could use a network

Networks are some of the most valuable type of businesses, but they are almost impossible to start directly. A network with no users has no value, and therefore cannot attract users. This is the classic cold start problem, and it kills most network-dependent startups before they ever get off the ground.

A good way to find a network idea is to look for tools that people already use alone, and ask whether the output of that tool would become dramatically more valuable if it were shared. Look at the software you use, the workflows you see, the things people create, and ask:

  • What tools do people use today where the output stays trapped on one person's computer or in one team's account?
  • What artifact does this tool naturally produce? (a file, a template, a board, a link, a dataset, a workflow)
  • What would change if that output were visible, shareable, or remixable by others? Does that artifact become substantially more valuable?
  • Is there a version of this tool you could build where sharing happens by default, not as an extra step, so that every new user automatically makes the product better for everyone else?
Pinterest
Founded 2009. $4.5Bn+ revenue, 600M MAUs.

Ben Silbermann, the founder of Pinterest, built what was essentially a personal bookmarking tool, aka a way to save images from the web into visual collections which he called boards. You could organize inspiration for a home renovation, collect recipe ideas, or curate a mood board. The artifact was the pin, an image saved to a board with a link back to its source. But the design contained a network as boards were public by default. When you pinned something, it appeared in feeds of people who followed you or browsed similar categories. When someone repinned your image to their own board, it spread further, creating a huge compounding loop.

Pinterest was founded in 2009, and reached 10 million monthly users faster than any standalone site in history at the time. Today, it has $4.5Bn+ revenue and 600M MAUs.

What comes after finding the idea?

Your idea is a starting point. Once you have it, the work of testing begins. What is the smallest thing you can do to validate whether this idea has potential? What is the riskiest assumption, the one that, if wrong, would kill the entire idea? Don't test the easy things. Test the thing that scares you.

The winning idea will often come not from the first hunch itself, but from the insights you uncover while testing a hunch that turns out to be wrong. The customer conversations you have, the objections you hear, the adjacent problems you discover: these are the raw material of iteration. The best founders move through a cycle of hunch → test → learn → updated hunch → test again, etc, and they do this faster than anyone else.

Hugging Face

Clément Delangue, Julien Chaumond, and Thomas Wolf founded Hugging Face out of Paris in Station F, as chatbot for teenagers, an AI companion that could hold casual conversations, understand emotions, and be entertaining. It was playful, almost frivolous. The chatbot gained traction but not massive retention.

In late 2018, two years after the team started the company, Google released BERT, a transformative new language model (for the time). But Google's release was inaccessible to most developers: it was super academic, locked to its own TensorFlow framework, and overall not a treat to use. The Hugging Face team, having already spent two years wrestling with exactly these kinds of models to build their AI chatbot friend, produced a clean, usable PyTorch implementation of BERT within a week. They open-sourced it.

The reaction from the ML community was massive, and that is the moment that clarified the company's actual direction. Their pivot was organic and progressive, based on the market pull they saw: they started sharing everything they'd learned about ML while building the chatbot, and within months they had become a go-to resource for the entire ML community. The chatbot was abandoned, and Hugging Face turned into the hub where developers share their models, datasets, and applications.

How to pick the right market

The main thing to understand about selecting the market to build your company in is that the market matters more than anything else. Market selection might be the most important thing that you have to get right.

You, nor anyone else in this world, including Sam Altman, can out-execute a market that doesn't exist or that is structurally not adapted for venture scale growth. Markets are a force in startups in the same way the law of gravity is a force in physics. You cannot escape them. You can't be an exception to the force of markets. Please remember this.

In our AI era, this gravity effect of markets is arguably stronger than ever before, because platform shifts compress time. Markets can appear, become crowded, and saturate within a single fund cycle, which punishes founders who rely on vibes instead of actual market pull and structural defensibility.

A startup's value is fundamentally tied to its market. Even the strongest team and product cannot win where customers aren't spending. As Sequoia warns, "Nature hates a vacuum, so why hasn't your solution been built before now?". Teams that understand and win a large, urgent market can achieve PMF almost by accident; conversely, teams in unready or tiny markets end up fighting for every customer. Marc Andreessen famously put it: in a great market with many willing buyers, "the market pulls product out of the startup," making everything else easier.

In short, get the market right or don't build.

We've covered how to generate ideas worth testing. Now the question is: is the market behind your idea any good? To help you figure this out, we've put together a handful of criteria that the best founders and investors in the world use to think about markets.

These should be used to evaluate which market you want to build in, and to give you transparency about how investors will think about your market. However, do remember that most investors - Tier1 included - get things wrong all the time. Use this checklist as a way to honestly evaluate yourself, and trust your own analysis, facts and data you gather from your customer discovery.

How to use this doc:

  • Use this to compare plausible markets that you could credibly pursue, and use it to exclude markets that look obviously bad.
  • During early customer discovery, update your beliefs about your market. Your goal is to replace narrative claims with factual observations and data, as you learn more about your market
  • Treat some criteria as fatal (eg, a very structurally capped number of buyers). Look for deal killers.
  • Inversely, look for one or two dimensions that could make a potential market unfairly good. Your market doesn't have to score well everywhere. But if you have a home run on one or two criteria, it's a good sign.
  • Your job, before you select a market, is to gain as much conviction as you possibly can on your market. If you're about 80% sure that you have a good market, go for it. You can never be fully sure.

ps: A Tier 1 VC will never, ever invest in your company, if they don't think that you are building in a market that can create at least a decacorn company.

Market pull: is this mission-critical?

This is the obvious one everyone repeats, but most founders still misunderstand it. Market pull is observable spending behavior (money, time, attention, risk) already being paid to make a critical problem go away. In a high-pull market, customers actively seek solutions.

In a pull market, customers have already admitted a problem exists by paying for substitutes (vendors, headcount, workarounds), and they are actively hunting for a better option. Ideally, the problem is actually attached to very large and renewable budgets. That can be consumer attention with massive scale, but more often it's business spend that expands with the economy (payments, compliance, engineering productivity, cloud/infra inputs, etc.).

Things you can test on your market:

  • Spend audit. List current alternatives (including DIY solutions) and estimate how much customers are spending on them (hours of labor, money, attention). Can you name the current budget line item (or shadow spend) you are replacing, and who signs for it? If you cannot point to credible spend, you are likely pushing. Today, pull is very easy to fake because everyone wants to run AI experiments. It's crucial to to distinguish pilot pull from production pull. The correct filter is whether budgets survive past the experiment stage. Retention is now more core than ever.
  • Inbound signals. If you've launched any early MVP or even a landing page, measure organic interest (sign-ups, pre-orders, viral interest) without paid ads. Are people contacting you or just ignoring? However, be wary that today, early inbound interest is cheap. What actually counts as pull, is whether customers are willing to convert past pilot to retention stage.
  • Ask yourself: if this pain is real and budgets exist, why hasn't the obvious solution already won? Your answer should name a constraint that recently broke (why now), or a structural reason incumbents can't/won't serve it (structure). If your answer is "execution", you're probably wrong.

Market trajectory: where is this market is going?

A great market doesn't have to be huge today. It does however have to become huge and inevitable tomorrow, with compounding demand and expanding budgets.

The right way to look at a market is never as a static number. The bet is always wether this market becomes huge in 5-10 years. Ask yourself how big it will be in 10 years, what forces make that growth likely, and why startups (not incumbents) get the right to win it.

The strongest framing treats TAM as a trajectory and a category destiny (if we win, what do we become synonymous with?)

Things you can test on your market:

  • Bottom-Up TAM. How many customers can exist, and what can they pay? How can each one of these grow? Sequoia famously looks at number of customers × revenue per customer, plus a narrative of how both figures can grow. It's also useful to measure via concrete adoption assumptions (eg what % of the Fortune 2000 could ultimately buy).
  • Understand market growth fundamentals. Write down the 2–3 forces that make the market expand regardless of you: cost curves collapsing, behavior shifts, regulation opening, new distribution surfaces. You should be able to name these specifically and have a credible hypothesis around why these change.
  • Argue how your product can drive a 10x (or, let's be ambitious, a 100x) increase in something (speed, cost reduction, usage) that justifies expanding the market. Step-change gains often unlock new customers or use cases. Anthropic, Lovable are all 100x improvement startups that can justify the heavy expansion of a market.

Market structure & profit potential: can anyone win big here?

There is a real and true distinction between market size and market quality. Some enormous markets are structurally awful: too much competition, no pricing power, or value captured in the wrong layer.

At its basic level, the question is: does the market reward the winner, and can the winner capture value profitably? As Peter Thiel argues, only markets where you can build a near-monopoly leads to sustained profits.

Markets differ in how value is distributed. Great markets allow a single (or few) companies to become highly profitable (think Google - or OpenAI & Anthropic), whereas perfect-competition markets leave no room economic profit.

  • To check profit potential, look at the concentration of buyers/suppliers, switching costs, network effects, and pricing power.
  • The most ideal market however allows a single company to become the default control point in the ecosystem. The question isn't only: can a company win this market? But rather: if a company wins, does the market structurally want the winner to become the default layer that everyone builds around? This is one of the clearest distinctions between a good market and a generational one. That is why Google search was so powerful: it was not just a product but the default interface to an entire (huge) category.

Look at:

  • Buyer power: many small buyers (good) vs few kingmakers (danger). Markets with a tiny number of powerful customers structurally cap upside and squeeze margins. If you are selling to a handful of buyers, you need an exceptionally strong wedge and a path to broaden demand.
  • Ask yourself: if we win, what do we become the default for? Strong answers sound like: the default payments layer for internet businesses, the default design environment for product teams with a clear explanation of why it is the default and not just one of many.
  • Check whether the market structurally wants coordination. Markets with network effects, standard-setting dynamics, workflow centralization, or developer ecosystems are much more likely to produce default winners.

Things you can test on your market:

  • Map the market's buyers and suppliers. Are there many small buyers or a few monopsonistic buyers? Are suppliers (if any) fragmented or consolidated? Prefer markets with fragmented customers and/or fragmented suppliers that you can aggregate or serve better.
  • Check if you have a real moat. List any natural moat (network effects, data advantage, regulatory barrier, distribution lock-in). If it's just "better UX," that's weak / please don't actually use this as a criteria to analyze a market; if it's "users won't switch because of a regulatory / data / distribution advantage", that's much better. Regarding technological moats: given model access is getting cheaper, model advantage is less structurally defensible than people think. Your moat must be where competition can't trivially copy: distribution, workflow embedding, proprietary data loops, trust/compliance posture, or a true network effect.
  • Ask whether suppliers / partners / adjacent tools would eventually have to build on top of you or route through you. If yes, that is a very strong sign. If all the other plays in the market can happily ignore you and still function, your market may support a good business but not a category-defining one.
  • In AI specifically, ask whether your product is just producing output, or whether it is becoming the place where the workflow itself lives. The latter is much more defensible.
  • Platform dependency risk. If you're building on model APIs, your product is exposed to deprecations and pricing changes. OpenAI explicitly states models / endpoints are regularly retired, with frequent forced migrations / sunsets. To build anything sustainable in this era, your structure has to be built around the possibility of vendor churn (multi-model support, eval harness).

Distribution asymmetry: does acquiring customers get cheaper over time?

Some markets are structurally much better than others because they contain an unfair distribution dynamic baked into the market itself. It means the route to growing in a market can compound, cheapen, or self-reinforce instead of remaining brute-force forever. And arguably, given the decrease in the cost of building product today, execution and speed around distribution becomes a more important differentiation point than ever before.

The strongest examples of modern decacorn outcomes usually have this feature. Stripe distributed through developers and developers could adopt it directly. Figma spread through individual designers and then teams before formal procurement caught up. Canva benefited from prosumer sharing, templates, and low-friction collaboration. Cursor is interesting for similar reasons: developer tools have a built-in distribution advantage when the user can adopt first and procurement follows later. The tool does not need to win an executive-level before it proves value. It proves value directly in use, which creates a massive asymmetry.

Things you can test on your market:

  • Check whether usage can naturally propagate inside a team, company, or network. Good signs are collaboration, file sharing, APIs, team workflows, standardized data, or any situation where one user naturally creates the conditions for more users.
  • Identify whether there is an existing distribution surface you can piggyback on: developer ecosystems, app stores, search, open-source communities, etc.
  • Be honest about whether your route to growth gets cheaper or more expensive over time. In the best markets, getting the first customers are hard, but later customers become substantially easier because ecosystem effects and product distribution compound. In weak markets, every customer costs as much effort as the previous one. Disregard word of mouth here, and try to think only about what is structural to the market and product.

Speed of iteration / clarity of feedback: can you learn fast in this market?

Measure how quickly you can learn if the market actually responds. Short feedback loops and low friction imply faster truth. For example, consumer or SMB SaaS markets often have faster PMF signals. Enterprise/government markets can work, but speed of learning is much slower. Sequoia's Jess Lee emphasizes learning velocity: teams learn in proportion to the feedback frequency. In some markets, truth emerges quickly (fast adoption, frequent usage, rapid cycles), while in others it emerges slowly (long procurement cycles, ambiguous user feedback).

The subtle point: speed isn't just about "growth." It is about whether the market gives clean signals. Can you see conversion? Can you see retention? Can you see expansion/usage? If the market's feedback is noisy (political buying, compliance delays, procurement cycles), your learning compounding breaks.

Things you can test on your market:

  • Cycle time. What is the time from "we send the MVP to a user" → "we get back measurable user behavior data" → "we build improvements from that data"? (If this loop is 6+ months by default, be wary.) If there is a repeatable way that you can shorten this loop for your company vs others who can't, this is also good.
  • How easily can a typical customer start using your product? (Self-serve sign-ups and trials score high; requiring months of integration or training scores low.) Young founders tend to fare much better in low friction signup environments, because you can out learn everyone by shipping and learning faster.

Market depth: does this market take you places?

Market depth means your wedge/beachhead sits inside a larger system where expansion is natural, because you accrue data/workflow/demand that unlocks adjacent products and segments. You start as a narrow solution, then you become a platform or infrastructure layer as you expand.

The right way to think about depth is: what do we get to own that makes the next product inevitable? Workflows, distribution, trust, compliance posture, developer mindshare, data exhaust, or a network.

Non-consensus thesis: is this market non-obvious?

The strongest markets usually look wrong at first, either too small, too early, or too crowded. The key is whether you can articulate a specific non-consensus belief that cashes out into inevitability.

The right question to ask yourself isn't is this crowded? It's: what do we believe that most smart people currently disbelieve, and what mechanism makes our belief become true? This is where the legendary elephants in mouseholes idea comes in: markets that look niche but can hide big spend.

For young founders, this matters because non-consensus is often the only way you get into decacorn space without a résumé. If you can't win through credibility, you win through being earlier and more right.

This is a little harder to test because it relies on a non-existent reality, but here are some things to think hard about:

  • Write down why most people ('experts', advisors, investors) would say this won't work or isn't big. Then counter each with your thesis. What is your unique insight or angle?
  • List incumbents or adjacent companies in the space. Why will your product win if those exist (again, "good UX" or "better integration with software X" is definitely not an solid answer). If the market is truly untapped, who is not solving it and why not?

Why now: if this is so great, what why hasn't anyone built it before?

Great startups are often bad ideas that became good ideas because a constraint broke: cost collapsed, distribution opened, regulation changed, or behavior shifted. Sequoia's why now prompt is the simplest forcing function: if the vacuum existed, why didn't someone fill it earlier?

Top why now mechanisms include: substantial cost collapse or price increase, emergence of a new distribution channel (eg mobile, eg new APIs), a new enabling technology, a new source of demand, a substantial change in regulation. Timing is not a trend (!) eg: consumers now care about thing x. Timing is a concrete change in feasibility.

The why now questions also determines whether you can out-run incumbents. If the platform shift is early, incumbents move slowly and often dismiss the market. That is when young teams can win: by being faster, more native, and more willing to rebuild workflows from scratch.

Things you can test on your market:

  • Ask yourself "Would this have plausibly failed 2 years ago?" If the honest answer is "no," your why-now is weak (and you're probably late). If the answer is "yes, because X wasn't possible," write X down and pressure test that X.
  • Identify the exact constraint that dropped. Write out the "before vs. after" world.

A few examples of great markets

Here are a few examples of companies where the market itself did a lot of the heavy lifting. The companies listed below all share three things:

  • They became decacorns
  • They were founded by young, non-obvious founders who did not rely on a massive network / fundraise / initial investment to get started
  • Their success can be largely explained by market structure as well as execution

Stripe

  • One of the strongest aspects of Stripe's market is that they were building the financial OS for internet-native businesses, or more specifically, the control layer over how money moves through software.
  • Market trajectory: Stripe effectively attached itself to the growth of internet GDP. As more companies moved online, as SaaS proliferated, and now as AI is, the total volume of online transactions increased massively. Stripe's revenue is a function of transaction volume, not just number of customers. That creates a structural dynamic where your TAM expands automatically without you doing anything, simply because the underlying economy is growing.
  • Distribution asymmetry: Stripe did not win through enterprise sales. Instead, it won through developers. The product could be integrated in minutes, tested immediately, and deployed without permission. That means the user and the buyer were often the same person. This created a bottom-up adoption loop where individual developers pulled Stripe into companies, and over time, those decisions hardened into company-wide standards. This is a fundamentally different distribution dynamic from selling to CFOs or procurement teams. It allowed two young founders with no network to compete against incumbents like PayPal.
  • Market depth: Stripe can expand naturally into every adjacent workflow where money touches systems. Beyond payments, they've built a revenue suite around billing, invoicing. 25% of all Delaware corporations are now created with Stripe Atlas. Stripe owns upstream company formation (Atlas), core transaction flow (payments), and downstream revenue workflows (billing).

Scale AI

  • Market pull: its extremely strong because the spend is not optional as it sits on a critical need of the AI platform shift: high-quality data and evaluation. If you are training models, you need labeled data and evaluation systems. This is not a nice to have but is on the critical path of product improvement. That creates a market where: budgets are large (AI labs, hyperscalers, governments), spend is recurring, demand increases as models improve
  • Trajectory: as models get larger, more complex, and more capable, the need for better/ mode diverse data, higher-quality evals and domain-specific feedback increases non-linearly. This creates a market where demand grows as a function of model capability, not just adoption. That is a deeper and more durable growth driver than most SaaS markets.
  • The speed of learning here are unusually strong because the buyers are often enterprise-sized in budgets but builder-speed in decision-making (frontier labs, hyperscalers, fast-moving AI teams). That matters for young founders: you can get large-ticket outcomes without multi-quarter sales processes if you're selling something that is obviously on the critical path of model improvement.
  • Market structure and position in the value chain: Scale sits inside the training and evaluation loop, which is a massive control point. Once embedded in the training/evaluation loop, switching costs become huge because workflows are customized, QA processes are tailored to their systems, benchmarks are built around your systems, and security and compliance layers are integrated. The more the customer uses you, the harder it becomes to leave which creates a super strong operational and infrastructural lock-in.

Cursor

  • Market pull: coding is already one of the most frequent, expensive, and measurable forms of work in most companies in the world today. Engineering budgets are huge, recurring, and directly tied to company output. If you can materially increase developer productivity, you are plugging directly into one of the largest cost centers in modern tech companies. What makes Cursor especially good is that it turns that pull into something users can verify almost instantly. A developer does not need to wait for for months to know whether Cursor is useful, but they can feel it in the first hour.
  • The market structure and default value capture potential are what make Cursor truly interesting. The reason investors got so excited is that the editor is the fundamental control point. If the winning product becomes the default place where engineers read, write, refactor, review, debug, and increasingly delegate work to agents, then value starts concentrating around that layer. Newer product launches are about automations, marketplaces, cloud agents, and JetBrains support. In other words, the company is not just trying to answer coding questions but is trying to become the operating system for everything related to software development. That's what we mean, when we say that Cursor could plausibly become the default for software development in a way that Github was previously. That is exactly the kind of market where a single company can become the default control point in the ecosystem rather than just one vendor among many.

Your customer is your (only) boss

The type of customer you sell to is a structural choice that shapes your entire company: what you build, how you sell, who you hire, how fast you learn, how much capital you need, and ultimately, how happy you'll be building the company. Their buying behavior, budget cycles, decision-making structure, and their tolerance for risk will impose constraints on everything you do.

Most founders think about this too late.

This document breaks down the four major customer types you're likely to encounter, what it means to build for each, and what your company looks like if you do. Use this to make an informed choice about what type of company you want to build, and to understand what you're signing up for.

Enterprise <3

Who they are: Large organizations, typically 1,000+ employees, $1B+ in revenue. Fortune 500 and Global 2000 companies.

The deal: Enterprise is where the biggest contracts live. Annual contract values (ACVs) of $100K–$1M+ are normal. A single customer can represent a meaningful fraction of your revenue.

Examples of enterprise-first startups: Snowflake ($3.46B revenue), Databricks ($5.4B revenue), CrowdStrike ($4B revenue), Palantir ($3B revenue), Datadog ($3B+ revenue), Veeva ($2.4B revenue), Scale AI, Anthropic, Celonis, UiPath, etc

What the sales cycle looks like

Enterprise sales cycles run from 3 months (if you're very lucky and / or very talented) to 18 months (more likely) from first contact to signed contract. You will deal with multiple stakeholders: the end user, their manager, the department head, IT security, procurement, legal, and often the CFO, CIO, CISO. Each has a different set of concerns and a different set of incentives. The end user cares about whether the product works. Procurement cares about price. Security cares about compliance. Legal cares about liability. You have to satisfy all of them, sequentially, and if any one of them says no, the deal stalls or dies.

Palantir is the extreme example. In its early days, Palantir would deploy engineers on-site for months, sometimes years, at customer locations, building custom integrations and proving value before a contract was even signed. The company ran at massive losses for over a decade, funded by investors who believed the lock-in from these deep deployments would eventually produce enormous, sticky revenue. They were right: Palantir's government contracts now run into the hundreds of millions, and its net dollar retention exceeds 120%. But this is a path that requires extraordinary patience and capital.

GTM

Enterprise GTM is relationship-driven and top-down. You need warm introductions, executive sponsors, and often a POC or pilot before anyone will sign and actual deal. The best enterprise startups find a way to make the initial deployment small and fast (a single team, a single use case) and then expand inside the organization. This is the land and expand model, and it works because it reduces the buyer's risk.

The hardest version of enterprise sales is when you're selling something that requires a new budget line item, aka something the company has never bought before. That's the situation Wiz faced entering cloud security. Their breakthrough was making the demo so fast and so visually compelling, surfacing vulnerabilities in a live 15-minute demo, that CISOs would pull in their teams immediately. They went from $0 to $100M ARR in 18 months. But even Wiz needed the underlying urgency: cloud security was already a board-level concern at most large companies.

Key metrics to track

In the first months (pre-revenue / first customers): measure pipeline and conviction signals. Track number of qualified conversations (are decision-makers taking meetings?), pilot conversion rate (do POCs turn into paid contracts?), time-to-first-deal (how long from first contact to signed contract?), and design partner depth (are early customers giving you real feedback, access and money?). The single most important early signal is whether a customer will commit budget, even a small pilot fee, before your product is fully built.

At scale: Net dollar retention (NDR) is the metric that matters most. It measures whether existing customers are spending more over time. Best-in-class enterprise companies run NDR above 130%, meaning even if you add zero new customers, your revenue grows 30% per year from expansion alone. Datadog runs above 120% NDR. If your NDR is below 110%, you have a retention or expansion problem. Other critical metrics: ACV, sales cycle length, win rate, etc

What your company looks like

Enterprise companies are sales-heavy. At scale, expect 25–40% of your headcount in sales, customer success, and solutions engineering. You'll need account executives who can run complex, multi-stakeholder deals, and they are expensive ($300K+).

What you need to be good at

Patience, relationship management, navigating complex organizations, and building trust. Enterprise buyers are fundamentally risk-averse (nobody ever got fired for buying IBM). Your job as a startup is to de-risk the purchase at every step. That means: create killer case studies, have safe references, invest time in compliance certifications, and be willing to do pilots and POCs that may not immediately convert.

Tech challenges that come with enterprise

  • Security reviews, compliance posture, admin controls, auditability (often the blocking constraint)
  • Integration into existing systems (identity, data pipelines, procurement systems)
  • Reliability and uptime obligations
  • Deployment complexity (on-prem, hybrid)

Fundraising implications

Enterprise-first startups typically need more capital upfront and earlier. You'll burn cash for 12–24 months before revenue becomes predictable, because long sales cycles mean your first deals take time to close and your first hires (senior AEs, solutions engineers) are expensive. The good news: once your playbook works, enterprise revenue is sticky, predictable, and expands, which makes subsequent fundraising significantly easier.

SMB

Who they are: Companies with fewer than 500 employees. Often owner-operated, with thin management layers and small (or nonexistent) IT departments. This includes restaurants, law firms, doctor and dental practices, construction companies, local retailers, professional services firms, and early-stage startups.

The deal: SMB contracts are small, typically $1K–$25K per year. But there are millions of them. The math of SMB is volume: you win by acquiring customers fast, retaining them efficiently, and keeping your cost to acquire and serve low.

Examples: Shopify ($11.5B+ revenue), Toast ($6B revenue), Square/Block ($22B revenue), HubSpot ($3B revenue), Monday.com ($1B ARR), Mindbody / Treatwell, Brex

What the sales cycle looks like

SMB sales cycles are short: days to weeks, rarely more than 2–3 months. Usually, the owner or a single decision-maker evaluates, decides, and pays, sometimes in the same call. There's no procurement department, no legal review, no security questionnaire. The decision is: does this solve my problem, and can I afford it? If yes, they'll sign up on the spot.

eg: ServiceTitan, which sells field service management software to plumbers, electricians, and HVAC contractors, built its early business through relentless outbound calling and trade show attendance. The founders grew up as sons of contractors and understood that these business owners made buying decisions fast: if you could show them on a 15-minute demo that your software would help them book more jobs and get paid faster, they'd sign that day. ServiceTitan reached over $600M in ARR by owning a vertical that most software companies dismissed as too blue-collar to bother with.

GTM

SMB GTM is high-velocity and product-led. Self-serve sign-up, free trials, and freemium models work because the decision-maker can evaluate the product themselves without needing an account executive. Marketing is content-driven, SEO-driven, and referral-driven.

The challenge with SMB is churn. Small businesses fail, change tools frequently, and are extremely price-sensitive. Annual SMB churn rates of 3–5% monthly (30–50% annually) are common and often structural. Aka, it's not that your product is bad, it's that small businesses are volatile. This means your acquisition engine has to run constantly just to replace lost customers, before you even start growing.

Key metrics

In the first months: The metrics that matter early are sign-up rate, activation rate (what % of sign-ups actually use the product meaningfully in their first week), and week-1/month-1 retention. You need to know whether people who try the product come back, before you worry about anything else. Also track time-to-value: how quickly does a new user reach the aha moment?

At scale: MRR/ARR growth, monthly and annual churn rate, CAC, CAC payback period (target under 6 months for SMB), and LTV:CAC ratio (target 3:1 or better).

What your company looks like

SMB companies are product and engineering heavy early on, then shift toward marketing as they scale. You rarely need expensive enterprise AEs; ideally, you need a self-serve funnel that converts with little human intervention.

Customer support is critical because SMB customers don't have IT departments, they call you when something breaks, and they expect fast answers. Shopify has thousands of support staff serving millions of merchants. The cost structure is driven by the volume of low-ACV relationships, each of which needs to be served efficiently.

What you need to be good at

Product simplicity, onboarding speed, and distribution efficiency. SMB buyers don't read whitepapers or sit through long demos. They need to understand what you do in 30 seconds, sign up in 2 minutes, and see value in the first session.

Fundraising implications

SMB startups are more capital-efficient early on: you can get to revenue fast, iterate on your sales motion with real customers, and demonstrate growth metrics within months. Seed rounds are typically sufficient to reach initial traction. The challenge comes at scale: because individual customers are low-value and churn is high, you need to spend continuously on acquisition just to maintain growth. This often means larger Series A/B rounds to fund marketing and sales infrastructure. Investors will scrutinize your unit economics, LTV:CAC and payback period more aggressively than they will for enterprise companies, because the margin for error is thinner.

Prosumer

Who they are: Individual professionals or small teams who adopt a product for their own work, often with their own money or a discretionary team budget, before the company formally procures it. The prosumer is a professional user acting like a consumer: they discover, evaluate, and adopt products on their own, then pull the organization along behind them.

The deal: This is the most powerful GTM motion of the last decade, and it has produced some of the most valuable software companies in history. Instead of selling to the company, you sell to the individual inside the company. They adopt your product because it makes them better at their job. Once enough individuals adopt, the company has no choice but to formalize the relationship, and that's when you start charging enterprise prices.

Notable prosumer-first startups: Figma ($1Bn+ revenue), Canva ($4B+ revenue), Cursor ($2B+ ARR), Slack (acquired for $27.7B), Notion ($600M+ ARR), Dropbox ($2.5B revenue), Zoom ($4.5B revenue), Airtable, Linear, Vercel, Loom, Postman, GitHub (acquired for $7.5B), Weights & Biases

What the sales cycle looks like

For the individual user, there is no sales cycle. They sign up, use the product, and decide within hours or days whether it's worth continuing. The "sales cycle" is really an activation and retention challenge: can you get them to the aha moment fast enough & is the product good enough? Figma, Notion, Cursor, Linear, and Vercel all follow this pattern. Nobody asks permission to use Cursor. A developer installs it, starts coding, feels the speed difference, and never goes back to VS Code. Weeks or months later, the engineering manager notices that half the team is using it and decides to standardize.

The enterprise sale still happens eventually, but by the time it does, the product is already embedded. 70% of Figma's enterprise deals originated from individual users on free or Pro plans who had already adopted the product organically. The enterprise sale is a formalization, not really a sale.

GTM

Product-led growth (PLG). The product is the primary acquisition channel. Distribution happens through usage: a designer shares a Figma link, a PM opens it, an engineer inspects it, and now three people are using Figma instead of one. Notion spreads through shared documents. Loom spreads through shared videos. The product carries the distribution.

The genius of this model is that it collapses CAC. Canva's acquisition cost per user is nearly zero for its free tier: users arrive through SEO, templates, and word of mouth. Conversion to paid happens when the user hits a natural upgrade trigger (needs brand kit, needs premium assets, needs team features). The entire funnel is instrumented inside the product, not inside a CRM.

But PLG has a hard requirement: the product must be genuinely, immediately useful to a single user without any network or any other users. If the product requires a team to be valuable, the cold-start problem kills you. That's why the prosumer wedge matters, it has to be valuable for one person, alone, on day one.

Key metrics

In the first months: Activation rate is everything. What percentage of people who sign up actually do the thing the product is designed for (create a design, write a document, run an experiment, ship code) within their first session? It should be at least above 30%. Also track time-to-wow-moment (how many seconds/minutes before the user sees the product's value?) and organic sharing rate (do users naturally send links, invite teammates, or share outputs without you prompting them?).

At scale: DAU/MAU ratio (daily engagement), free-to-paid conversion rate (typically 2–5% for freemium, 10–25% for free trial), expansion revenue (do teams grow into enterprise contracts?), and virality coefficient (does one user's usage naturally bring in more users?). The transition from prosumer to enterprise revenue is the hardest part: track what percentage of your revenue comes from self-serve vs sales-assisted, and how that ratio shifts over time.

What your company looks like

Prosumer companies are engineering and product heavy. Growth teams, product analytics, and design are the critical functions. You'll eventually need enterprise sales as you move upmarket, but the initial motion is product-driven.

The workforce split looks more like a consumer tech company than an enterprise one: heavy on engineering, product, and design; lighter on sales and marketing. Notion had only 12 employees when it rebuilt its product. Cursor had less than 30 people when it hit 100M revenue. The leverage comes from the product, not the salesforce.

What you need to be good at

Product craft, onboarding design and all-around design, and growth instrumentation. You need to be obsessive about the first-use experience: every second of friction between sign-up and the aha moment costs you users. You also need to design natural product upgrade triggers that feel like real unlocks and must-haves. And you need to build the product so that usage naturally creates sharing (collaboration links, shared files, embedded content).

Tech challenges

  • UX quality is existential
  • Collaboration and sharing surfaces (often real-time)
  • Template/content systems (distribution often runs through libraries or marketplaces)
  • Performance and reliability at scale (if it's consumer-ish adoption volume)

Fundraising implications

Prosumer/PLG is the most capital-efficient path in the early stages. You can build a product with a tiny team, acquire users for near-zero cost through organic channels, and demonstrate PMF through usage and retention data long before you need a sales team. This makes seed and pre-seed fundraising relatively straightforward: investors can see real usage data. The capital need increases when you start building the enterprise sales motion on top of the PLG base: hiring AEs, building security/compliance features, and supporting large customer deployments. Like SMB startups, many PLG companies raise larger rounds at Series A/B specifically to fund this expansion.

Consumer

Who they are: Individuals spending their own money (or attention) on products for personal use. This includes social media, entertainment, fintech, health, education, etc

The deal: Consumer is the highest-risk, highest-reward category. The potential scale is enormous (billions of people) but so are the challenges. Consumer products compete for the scarcest resource in the world: human attention. You have to be not just good but irresistible, because the switching cost is zero and the competition is infinite. A user can delete your app in two seconds and never think about you again.

Examples of consumer startups: Spotify ($19B revenue), Duolingo ($1B revenue), WhatsApp (acquired for $19B), Instagram (acquired for $1B, now worth $100B+), TikTok ($35B+ revenue), Revolut ($4B revenue), Monzo, Oura, Discord

What the sales cycle looks like

There is no sales cycle. There's a download (or a click), a first impression, and either you hook them in the first 30 seconds or they're gone. Consumer products live and die by activation and retention, measured in seconds.

GTM

Consumer GTM is distribution-first. You need to reach millions of people, which means you need either virality, paid acquisition at scale, or platform distribution (App Store, Google Play, social media, SEO). Organic virality is the holy grail: products that spread because using them inherently involves sharing (eg WhatsApp). But most consumer products can't rely on virality alone and need to supplement with paid acquisition, which gets expensive fast.

The cost of acquiring a consumer user varies wildly, from near-zero for viral products to $5–$50+ for paid acquisition in competitive categories (dating, fintech, health). The challenge is that consumer LTVs are often low ($20–$200 per year for subscription products), which means your unit economics are unforgiving. If your CAC exceeds a few dollars and your retention is mediocre, you burn cash at an alarming rate.

Spotify solved the acquisition problem by offering a genuinely excellent free tier that served as its own distribution engine. Free users listened to ad-supported music, which was good enough to create habit, and a meaningful percentage converted to Premium to remove ads and unlock offline listening.

Key metrics

In the first months: D1 and D7 retention are the only metrics that matter. D1 retention = what percentage of people who use the product today come back tomorrow. D7 = what percentage come back a week later. Aim for D1 above 40% and D7 above 20%. Also track session length and frequency: are users spending meaningful time, and are they opening the app on their own (not just from a push notification)?

At scale: DAU/MAU is the single most important consumer metric. It tells you what fraction of your users are engaged on any given day. Social media apps target 50%+. Messaging apps can exceed 70%. A productivity app at 20% is struggling. Other critical metrics: D30 retention, session length, session frequency, viral coefficient (K-factor), CAC, LTV, and for subscription products, free-to-paid conversion and monthly churn. Consumer LTV:CAC ratios need to be at least 3:1, and payback should be under 6 months given how volatile consumer attention is.

What your company looks like

Consumer companies are product and engineering dominated, with large growth/marketing teams. The workforce split is typically 40–60% engineering and product, 20–30% marketing and growth.

What you need to be good at

Product intuition, speed of iteration, and understanding of human behavior. Consumer is the category where taste matters most. You're not selling a solution to a business problem, you're competing for a person's time against everything else they could be doing: watching Netflix, scrolling Instagram, or just going to sleep. Your product should provide delight and addiction.

Consumer is also the hardest category for young founders to raise money in, because the failure rate is extremely high and the signals of success (retention curves, engagement metrics) take time to materialize. Investors know that most consumer products never find PMF, and the ones that do often can't monetize. If you're going consumer, you need to show an extraordinary vision and / or an extraordinary early retention. Not just downloads, not just sign-ups, but people coming back day after day without prompting.

Sometimes, people say that building in consumer is like throwing spaghetti at a wall and seeing what sticks - but your spaghetti has less than 0.0000001% chance of sticking.

Tech challenges

  • Design quality and polish
  • Scaling infrastructure and latency (consumer usage is spiky and unpredictable). Serving billions of requests per day, personalizing content in real time, and keeping infrastructure costs manageable.
  • Fraud, abuse, and adversarial behavior (especially in social, fintech, and marketplace products)

Fundraising implications

Consumer startups face a fundraising paradox: you often need significant capital to reach the scale where unit economics work, but investors are reluctant to fund consumer because the failure rate is so high. Pre-seed and seed rounds are achievable on vision and early prototypes, but Series A typically requires strong retention data: investors want to see flattening retention curves (D30+ stabilizing) before they'll commit meaningful capital.

Consumer fintech is an exception: companies like Revolut and Monzo raised large rounds early because the revenue model (interchange, lending, subscriptions) is clearer and the regulatory moats are substantial.

Which customer type is right for you?

You should ask yourself where you are best positioned to enjoy building, and to win.

A few things to have in mind:

  • If you are a young founder with no enterprise network, enterprise-first is usually a tough choice. You don't have the relationships, the credibility, or the runway to survive 12-month sales cycles with zero revenue. Prosumer/PLG or SMB will give you faster feedback, faster revenue, and faster learning. You can move upmarket later: Figma, Datadog, Slack, and Cursor all started bottoms-up and grew into enterprise. However, if you do have a substantial network in an industry and / or you're an exceptional sales person, you may want to consider enterprise.
  • If you're going consumer, be honest about the stakes. Consumer is the category where the outcomes are most binary: you either find PMF and grow explosively, or you don't and you die (which is very, very likely). There is very little middle ground. The competition for attention is brutal, the bar for quality is impossibly high, and most consumer startups never find retention. If you go this route, you need to be obsessive about the first-use experience and willing to iterate on it relentlessly.

Customer discovery

Customer discovery means talking to the people you think you're building for to better understand what they want. It sounds simple but it really isn't. Humans are polite and confusing, founders are biased, and there is a LOT of noise to cut through to get to some form of signal.

The main thing to know is that customer discovery needs a system to be efficient. This doc gives you the system, and shares tips and tricks that have worked for other founders.

Customer discovery is about validating 5 things, in the following order

Once you've had a bunch of initial conversations, and narrowed in on a problem space you want to build in, and a general idea of the problem you want to solve; you can start building your system for customer discovery.

When you're doing customer discovery, every step is interdependent with the other - aka, your problem depends on your ICP, your price depends on your problem, etc. Hence, there is a correct sequence to test when doing customer discovery. Each step should be validated before you invest heavily in the next.

Step 1: ICP

Once you've had enough conversations with different players within a single problem-space (20–40+), patterns will start to emerge. Some people you talk to will lean forward, get excited, and want to keep talking. Others will nod politely and won't commit. The ones who lean forward are your ICP (ideal customer profile). Your job is to figure out what they have in common.

What is an ICP?: "Small businesses", "developers", "CPG enterprise companies" is not an ICP. Your ICP needs at least three specific, measurable attributes that let you find these people in the real world and tell immediately whether someone fits or not. The best founders get absurdly narrow at the start. The goal is to expand later, once you've won the wedge.

  • Gusto started with six ICP attributes: companies with five or fewer employees, in California, offering no benefits, with only salaried employees, no contractors, and willing to wait eight days after running payroll to get paid.
  • Snyk started with: developers building with Node.js who were security-conscious.

You obviously won't start this narrow from Day 1, but your goal is to get to this precision eventually.

How do you find your ICP? Talk to 20-40+ people who you think might have the problem. Look for who leans in. Look at: which people got most excited? What do they have in common? Which people said "I want this now" versus "that's interesting"? Which people asked about pricing, unprompted?

When you find a cluster of people who all respond with real enthusiasm, and they share identifiable traits (industry, company size, role, specific technology, specific pain point), that's your ICP.

Step 2: problem

Once you've identified who you're building for, understand exactly what they're struggling with: what it actually means for them, in their words, in their workflow, in their daily reality.

What does the problem look like, in practice? How much is it costing them? Go deep in your understanding of the impact / cost of the problem, and exactly how it shows up in your users' and customers' workflows.

Step 3: promise

Before you build anything, frame a compelling promise of how you'll take your customer from their pain to a better outcome. The promise is not a feature list but the transformation you want to make happen: "You currently spend 5 days waiting for bacterial test results. With us, you get results in 10 minutes." That's Spore.Bio's promise.

Good promises are quantified: a percentage improvement, a time reduction, a cost saving, a risk eliminated. Your promise should be compelling enough that someone who has the problem can immediately assess whether it would change their life.

Test the promise in your conversations before you build anything. Watch for the reactions. If you can't get at least a few people to say something like "when can I have this?" from the promise alone, you have an issue with your promise / problem, not with your product.

Step 4: price

This is where most founders flinch because they think it feels premature to talk about money before you've built anything. But testing willingness to pay is one of the most important things you can do early, because it separates real demand and market pull from just polite curiosity.

You don't need a finished product to test pricing. You need a clear enough promise that someone can evaluate whether it's worth paying for. Then, use a commitment test and gradually test more specific pricing points: "Would you sign a letter of intent?" "Would you put down a deposit for early access?" "Would you pay for a pilot?"

A useful hack from Michael Sippey (former VP Product, Twitter): throw out a deliberately high number. "What if it cost $1,000?" Watch their reaction. They'll often reflexively say something like "No way, I'd never spend more than $150 on that." And there's your price anchor. Test higher and higher pricing with next customers until you hit a definite ceiling.

Step 5: product

Only once you've validated steps 1 through 4, you should invest in building product more seriously. It must be the right product for the right problem, for the right customer, at the right price.

This doesn't mean you can't use a mock-up or a very simple MVP earlier to test your promise or make conversations more concrete, but don't invest and substantial time nor money before you're confident on steps 1-4.

And now, iterate

The journey of validating the right ICP, problem, promise, price, and product is iterative, not linear. You will not get each step right on the first try. The goal is to be wrong in a way that teaches you something, and to learn faster than everyone else. Keep a log: what did you believe going in, what did you learn, what changed. Update as you go.

Tips for how to do this in practice

The core principle: extract behavior, not opinions

Rob Fitzpatrick who wrote the definitive book on this (The Mom Test), puts it bluntly: if you're getting compliments in your customer interviews, you're doing it wrong. What counts as data is behavior, concrete things people have actually done in the past. Behavior is factual. If you ask 100 people what they did yesterday when they encountered a specific problem, you'll get roughly the same answer from all of them. If you ask 100 people what features they'd like, you'll get 100 different answers, none of which are reliable.

The rule: talk about their life, not your idea. Ask about what they've done, not what they'd do. Ask about the past, not the future. Ask about specifics, not generalities.

Bad questions:

  • "Do you think this is a good idea?" (leading, opinion, yes/no)
  • "Would you use a product that does X?" (hypothetical)
  • "What features would you want?" (hypothetical, and they'll invent things they don't actually need)

Good questions & five questions you can always ask:

These five questions, adapted from YC's Diana Hu, work at every stage of customer discovery. They are a reliable backbone for any conversation.

1. "What is the hardest part about doing X?" This gets to the core pain. You want to find situations that occur frequently enough or are painful enough to be worth solving.

2. "Tell me about the last time you encountered that problem." This forces the person to give you a specific, detailed story rather than a generalization. While you're listening, you may not realize what's important, but when you review your notes later, gold nuggets will appear. This question gives you context about how the problem shows up in their actual life.

3. "Why was that hard?" This is the question that gives you your marketing copy. When people explain why something is hard, they use the language your future customers will use. They tell you what they care about. Pay close attention to the exact words they use, you should reuse them.

4. "What, if anything, have you done to try to solve the problem?" This question does two things. First, it reveals your actual competitors (not the ones you imagined, but the ones your users are actually using). Second, it qualifies how serious the user is. If someone is actively trying to solve the problem, even with duct tape and spreadsheets, that's a strong signal. If they've never bothered to look for a solution, the problem probably isn't painful enough.

5. "What don't you love about the solutions you've tried?" This is better than asking "what features would you want?" because it's grounded in real experience. The person tells you specifically what's broken about their current workaround, which gives you a starting point for your feature set that's rooted in reality rather than imagination.

How do I structure each customer discovery conversation?

Prepare a plan for every conversation. You only need 15–30 minutes, and you should be listening at least 80% of the time.

  • Zoom out → Zoom in → Zoom out. Start broad (how do they work, what does their day look like, what tools do they use), then narrow to the specific problem area you care about, then zoom back out to ask what matters most overall. This structure prevents you from anchoring the conversation too early and missing adjacent problems that might be more important than the one you came in to discuss.
  • Set a goal before each conversation. You need to know, before you pick up the phone, what you're trying to do. There are two types of interview goals:
    • Learning goal: a specific fact you want to uncover. Example: "How do they currently monitor efficienct?" or "Who has the budget authority to buy this?"
    • Commitment goal: a concrete next step you want the person to take. Example: a paid pilot, an introduction to a colleague, permission to observe their workflow, or simply agreeing to a second conversation.

If your conversation doesn't end with either a new fact that changes or confirms your current understanding, or a concrete commitment, it was probably wasted.

  • Debrief after each conversation. Do not let the insight fade. Within 15 minutes of ending a conversation, sit down (ideally with your cofounder) and do a quick debrief on three things:
    • Data: What specific facts did you learn? What did the person say (direct quotes) and show (body language, tone, emotion)? What confirmed your hypothesis? What contradicted it?
    • Insights: What interpretation can you draw? What patterns are emerging across conversations? Be honest about whether the pattern is real or whether you're seeing what you want to see.
    • Action: What specifically will you change as a result of this conversation? A question to ask next time? A new ICP hypothesis?

At the end of each week, do a longer synthesis. Look across all the conversations from that sprint. Which themes keep repeating? Where are there contradictions? What surprised you?

How do I find the people to talk to?

Start with people you can actually reach. Five deep conversations with the right people teach you more than fifty shallow ones with random strangers.

  • Start on easy mode with low hanging fruit. Talk to friends, former colleagues, and people in your network who are close to the problem. These conversations will be lower-stakes and will help you practice before you talk to the people who really matter.
  • Then graduate to cold outreach. LinkedIn messages, email, and X DMs all work if you're specific about why you're reaching out and what you want.
  • Go where your customers naturally gather. Find the conferences, Slack communities, subreddits, trade shows, or physical locations where your target customers spend time, and show up.

How many conversations do I need to have?

You can get remarkably strong signal from 5–8 deep conversations with the same type of person, as long as you're focused on one specific question or hypothesis per sprint. Plan for at least 25–40 conversations total, focussing on different areas of discovery.

How do I know if the customers are speaking to are really serious?

The hardest lesson in customer discovery is learning to distinguish genuine interest from polite encouragement. Most of the signals you receive will be encouraging but meaningless. The most important thing to understand is that commitment is measured by what someone gives up, not what they say. Time, money, and social capital (introducing you to their boss) are the only real currencies. Money is much better than time and social capital. Be blunt about this, and don't fool yourself.

Ask the question you're terrified to ask. "Will you sign this contract today?" If they say no, that's data. "Can you introduce me to your VP?" If they hesitate, that's data. The answers that make you uncomfortable are the ones that move you forward.

Bad signals: "Send me the link when it's ready." "We should definitely stay in touch."

Good signals involve real cost aka money (ideally), time, or reputation:

  • They agree to a paid pilot, even a small one
  • They introduce you to other people in their company or network
  • They share proprietary data or give you access to their systems
  • They carve out time for a follow-up meeting and put it on the calendar
  • They ask "how much does it cost?" without you bringing up pricing
  • They start describing their implementation plan before you've even finished the demo

When am I "done" with customer discovery?

Customer discovery is never truly finished. But there are clear signals that you've done enough to move forward with conviction:

  • You can describe your ICP with at least three specific attributes, and you've validated it with at least 5–10 conversations with people who match
  • You can name the specific problem you're solving, in the exact words your customers use to describe it
  • You know what your customers are currently doing to solve the problem (their workarounds, competitors, and duct-tape solutions)
  • You know what they'd pay, not because they told you "sure, I'd pay for that" (which is hypothetical) but because you can see what they're already spending on alternatives, and they've agreed to a specific number
  • At least two people from the same ICP have given you a real commitment: a paid pilot, a signed LOI, a deposit, or at the very minimum, dedicated time and access to help you build the right thing

If you have all of these, you're ready to build. If you're missing any of them, keep talking. The conversations you skip now are the months you'll waste later building the wrong thing.

Effective outreach

Outreach is a pipeline you build to repeatedly to reach the right people, run good conversations, and convert the best ones as customers.

This doc is a playbook for helping you efficiently build and run this pipeline across warm network, intros, cold outreach, and more creative approaches.

Mental models

Outreach is annoying and painful but incredibly high leverage. Do not outsource it in early stages.

High-quality early outreach is manual, long, and painful. But it's your job as a founder, and under no circumstances should you outsource or try to automate this process in early stages. It's often when you do outreach, that you learn key information about the market pull and about your ICP, promise, pricing etc.

As an early-stage founder, nobody knows your company and your brand opens zero doors. But when the outreach comes from a founder who has clearly done the research, who understands the customer's problem, who is willing to give their personal phone number and say "call me anytime": that works in a way no sales team can replicate. People are making a bet on you personally, not on your company. You need to lean into that and put your name, your story, and your personal commitment in every interaction. The emails you send this week may produce the relationships that shape your entire company. Treat them accordingly. You only need one really good first customer to get things off the ground.

Treat outreach like a measurement system

Your should really think of outreach as a measurement system for do people care enough about my problem? In Sequoia's PMF pressure test, one of the earliest positive indicators is a high hit rate on cold outreach, because it's closer to truth than friendly responses from people predisposed to be nice.

That's why you should treat outreach like an experiment with inputs and outputs, and data signals to measure efficacy:

Inputs you control:

  • Targeting (who you reach aka how on point is your ICP)
  • Value proposition (what you're offering aka how good is your promise)
  • Friction (how hard it is to respond aka how well crafted is your outreach message)
  • Channel (email, phone, LinkedIn, community, in-person)
  • Execution (follow-ups, iteration frequency)

Outputs you can measure:

  • Opens / acceptance (weak signal; often just curiosity)
  • Replies (stronger)
  • Meetings that happen (strong)
  • Depth of engagement (very strong: do they bring data, invite teammates, share docs, ask for next steps?)
  • Commitment (strongest: pilot, LOI, paid trial, intro to decision maker)

How to run this funnel:

Build the funnel backwards (this is the non-negotiable mental model). Start from "I need 3 pilots in month 1" and work backward: how many meetings, replies, and targeted messages does that require given your conversion rates? What can you test to optimize conversion rates? If the conversion rates are terrible, are you sure you're solving the right problem?

Step 1: start with low hanging fruit (= your existing network)

Your first conversations should come from people who you already know - either that are directly relevant to your problem space, or who can introduce you to people that are. These people will not necessarily become your customers (they probably won't), but they will allow you to learn, and hopefully, eventually, lead you to actual customers.

Who to reach out to first: start with people you know from the obvious networks. Former colleagues and managers from any job you've held; uni classmates, professors, and alumni networks; friends and family who work your target market; fellow founders (especially from The Bridge, EF, or other programs) - they tend to get the pain and respond well; people you've interacted with on X, LinkedIn, etc.

If you're asking to be connected to someone, write a good, forwardable email to minimize friction. The best practice is double opt-in intros: the connector asks the recipient if they want the intro before connecting. This is where most founders shoot themselves in the foot: they make the intro a writing project for the connector. Instead, send a crisp, forwardable email addressed to the final recipient, so the introducer can forward in 15 seconds without any hassle at all and minimal cognitive load on the connector.

The correct process for requesting an introduction:

  1. Identify the specific person you want to meet. "I'd love to connect with Sarah B, VP Engineering at Datadog" not "can you connect me with someone at Datadog."
  2. Ask the introducer if they'd be willing to make this specific connection. Tell them you'll write a separate email they can simply forward; all they'll need to do is add a line and hit send.
  3. Write a self-contained forwardable email. This is a brand new email (not a reply in the existing thread) with a custom subject line, your company blurb, and a specific call to action. It should be readable in under 45 seconds and forwardable from a phone. Most likely, the introducer will forward it with their own note and are not going to CC you blindly. They'll ask the recipient if they're open to connecting.
  4. When connected, respond immediately, within half an hour if possible. Speed signals seriousness to both the recipient and the introducer.
  5. Close the loop with the introducer. After the meeting happens, tell them how it went. This is common courtesy, and it makes them more likely to intro you again.

A good forwardable intro email has:

  • a credibility hook (relevant background, something nice you share in common, edge, traction, customers)
  • 2–4 sentences "what we do + why you're reaching out"
  • a very low-friction CTA ("open to 15 min next week?" / "can I send 3 questions over email?")

The networking multiplier: At the end of every single conversation you run, ask: "Is there anyone else you think I should talk to about this?" This one question consistently produces the highest-quality leads in early-stage outreach because it comes with a warm introduction attached. If someone gives you a name, ask them to make the intro rather than reaching out cold and saying "so-and-so suggested I contact you". The warm handoff will convert higher than a cold email that merely name drops the referrer.

Step 2: cold outreach

Once you've dried out warm conversations, you should have a clearer sense of who you're looking for. Now it's time to reach strangers. Most cold emails are generic, self-centered, too long, and obviously automated. If you write a short, personalized, human message that demonstrate you've actually thought about the recipient, you will get responses (assuming you put the reps in).

How to write a good cold emails

  • Keep it (very) short. Five to seven sentences maximum. Your email should be readable on a phone screen without scrolling. If someone has to invest cognitive effort to figure out what you want, they'll archive it.
  • Make a specific, low-friction ask - have a single, specific goal. "Could I get 15 minutes this week to learn how your team handles X?". The ask should be so small that saying yes is easier than thinking about it.
  • Write like a human, not a company. Use your personal name as the sender. Write "hi" not "Dear Madam." Use lowercase, use contractions, write the way you'd text a colleague.
  • Establish credibility in one line. The recipient needs a reason to take you seriously before they'll read further and give you their time. Write a short sentence to establish yourself as credible: "I'm a former ML engineer at Hugging Face, now building X" or "I'm a founder working on X" or "I've spent 6 years in food safety testing." The best credibility signals are institutional brands (employers, universities, accelerators), hard things you've done, or people they know.
  • Make it about them. The email should be about the reader, not about you. "I've been talking to factory managers who tell me contamination testing takes 5 days and costs them X per incident. I'm exploring whether there's a way to cut that to minutes by using new technology X." This tells the recipient you understand their world and invites them to engage on something they care about.
  • Follow up. Most people don't respond to the first email because they're busy. Plan to follow up 2–3 times, spaced a few days apart. Keep follow-ups even shorter: "Just bumping this, would love 15 minutes if you have time this week." Don't get frustrated if someone doesn't respond (nobody owes you anything).
  • Show extra effort, be creative (but not crazy). The fastest way to get replies is to do part of the job before they respond. For example, you could build a short loom-style video showing how you'd solve some part of their problem.

Finding email addresses

This is a solved problem. Use tools like Apollo.io etc find professional email addresses. LinkedIn Sales Navigator lets you filter by company, title, seniority, and geography. You can often guess email formats (firstname@company.com or firstname.lastname@company.com) once you know the pattern at a target company.

LinkedIn outreach

LinkedIn works differently from email. Connection requests with no message attached actually have a higher acceptance rate than those with a long note. Once someone accepts your connection, then send a short, personalized message. A few things to have in mind:

  • Volume limits: with a free account, send no more than 20–30 connection requests per day. Going higher triggers LinkedIn's spam detection and can get your account restricted. Withdraw pending requests older than 10–15 days.
  • Keep messages short. LinkedIn messages between 200–400 characters have the highest response rates. This is roughly 2–3 sentences. Treat it like a text message.
  • LinkedIn Sales Navigator is worth the investment: advanced filters for title, seniority, company size, and technology stack.

X outreach

For certain audiences (developers, founders, designers), X DMs can be more effective than email. You should engage with their public content first (reply to tweets, quote-tweet with a thoughtful addition), then DM with a short, specific ask. The advantage of X is that your public presence serves as credibility. If you've been sharing interesting thoughts about the problem space, the recipient can quickly assess whether you're worth talking to.

Step 3: go where your customers live - communities, conferences, etc

The highest-quality outreach often isn't an email or a DM at all. It's showing up in the physical or digital spaces where your target customers already spend time, and starting conversations face-to-face.

In-person events

Trade shows, conferences, industry meetups, and professional gatherings are goldmines for early-stage founders. The conversion rate from an in-person conversation is dramatically higher than from any digital channel, because you get the full context, body language, tone, real-time reactions, and because people are in a mode where they expect to meet new people. The common pattern: go to where your customers physically are, bring something (value, food, a demo, something fun), and start conversations.

  • Tracy Young, cofounder of PlanGrid (construction software, acquired by Autodesk for $875M), went to construction sites and brought doughnuts. She didn't have an app. She didn't have a pitch deck. She had doughnuts and questions. She'd walk up to workers and site managers and ask how they handled blueprints. These conversations shaped the product that construction workers actually wanted to use.
  • Scale AI's founding team went to CVPR (a major computer vision conference) with laptops, pitching their data labeling service from booth to booth. They didn't have a booth of their own. They just showed up and talked to every company building autonomous vehicles, face-to-face, about their data labeling pain.
  • Uber's early team used street teams at the Caltrain station, handing out referral codes. Travis Kalanick personally went to Twitter's headquarters and distributed codes.

Online communities

Many customer segments have rich online places where they hang out, discuss problems, share solutions, and evaluate tools. These are free intelligence sources and outreach channels if you use them well.

  • Reddit and Hacker News: classic gold-mine. If your customers are developers, technical leaders, or startup founders, these communities are essential. Participate genuinely: answer questions, share insights, contribute value.
  • Slack and Discord communities: Many industries have active Discord channels / Slack groups (SaaS, DevOps, marketing, fintech, AI/ML). Join them and read, listen. Then engage when you have something genuinely useful to contribute.
  • Industry-specific forums and groups: Construction has its forums. Manufacturing has its trade publications. Finance has its LinkedIn groups. Whatever industry you're targeting, find where practitioners talk to each other and start participating.

Content as outreach

Writing publicly about the problem you're solving is one of the most effective long-term outreach strategies. Blog posts, X threads, LinkedIn posts, and even short videos that demonstrate deep understanding of your target customer's problems will attract exactly the people you want to talk to.

How much outreach is enough outreach?

Most founders dramatically underestimate the volume of outreach required, and give up / discard a problem space just because they haven't put the outreach reps in. A typical (successful!) B2B outreach funnel could be: if you send 800 emails, roughly 400 get opened (50%), 40 generate responses (10%), 10 convert to demos (25%), and 1 becomes a customer (10% or 0.125% of your original 800). That means you need to be sending dozens of emails per day, not a handful per week.

At the early stage, you should be blocking off 3-4 hours per day for outreach only. Track everything. Measure open rates, response rates, and conversion to meetings. If your open rates are below 40%, your targeting or subject lines are off. If your response rates are below 10%, your email copy or value proposition needs work. If people take meetings but don't convert to next steps, your product or promise isn't resonating.

Start manual. Write every email yourself. Once you've found copy that consistently generates responses, then you can start to templatize and scale. But never automate before you've validated the message by hand. Your early emails will be bad but that's fine. Iterate. Each batch should be slightly different based on what you learned from the last one.

Founder stories to get you inspired on outreach

Stripe. Stripe's founders didn't wait for people to sign up. When they met a founder at any event who expressed interest, Patrick Collison would say "give me your laptop" and set up Stripe integration on the spot. By the time the conversation was over, the founder was a Stripe customer. This eliminated every source of friction between interest and adoption.

Pinterest's Ben Silbermann used to walk by the Apple Store, change all the display computers to show Pinterest, then stand in the back and watch people react.

Photoroom's founders used to go to McDonalds, and pay for a free hamburger to the people willing to test their app as they were in line or eating.

Snyk ran vulnerability checks on prospects before reaching out: "I found 5 unprotected endpoints on your public API: here's the list and what I'd recommend." For your first 10–20 targets, it converts at a dramatically higher rate than any email.

How to introduce yourself so that everyone listens

Sometimes, your introduction will be the most important two minutes of your early founder life. You will give this intro hundreds of times: to potential cofounders, customers, investors, people you want to hire, etc. You should spend time drafting this intro, so that it lands and makes you absolutely memorable. The goal is to be so clear and so specific that people remember you, and want to listen to what you have to say out of a sea of people.

The European intro vs the US intro

Charlie Songhurst is a great friend and investor of Entrepreneurs First and The Bridge, and you'll likely get to meet him as part of your journey with us. Charlie is English but has been living in San Francisco for decades. He often helps explain the importance of "the intro", and the difference in communication style between what to say in Europe vs in the US. Here's how he frames it using his own background:

Charlie's UK intro: "Hello, my name is Charlie Songhurst. After reading PPE at Oxford, I started out my career at McKinsey in London where I worked with several enterprise clients on various costly subjects. Afterwards, I joined Microsoft where I headed strategy in the UK. I then moved to San Francisco where I now still live, and where I angel invest in early-stage companies of all different types of industries. I also sit on the board of Meta."

Charlie's US intro: "Hi, I'm Charlie Songhurst. After studying at Oxford, I got my first job at McKinsey earning $200K a year. Two years later, after writing a strategy document that got picked up by Steve Ballmer, I was offered to join Microsoft as GM London on a $2M yearly salary, eventually becoming Head of Corporate Strategy where I tried to buy Yahoo. I invested $100k in Bitcoin in 2013, and when it exploded I became a billionaire a few years later. I'm now one of the world's most prolific angel investors - to date I've made over 600 investments from my base in San Francisco. At the start of 2025 I was appointed to the Board of Meta where I advise Mark Zuckerberg on Meta's AI strategy and other big topics. I've seen it all. It's great to meet you."

We're not all Charlies (yet). But look at how much more impactful the US version of his intro is. The first version is a chronological recitation of job titles: polite, accurate, and completely forgettable. The second uses numbers, specifics, and outcomes. And notice: there are almost no adjectives. No "I was fortunate to" or "I had the privilege of." Just facts, data, and a more compelling and precise way to structure the same story.

When you craft your intro, try to lead with the strongest thing, not the first thing. Most people default to chronological order: school, first job, second job, current thing. But if the most impressive fact about you is that you built a product used by 2 million people, say that first. You earn each additional second of someone's attention. If you bury your best material at the end, they've likely already tuned out.

Also, try to be yourself and add your flavor. That's what will make your intro uniquely you, which will likely be more memorable.

Building your co-founding team

Finding a great co-founder is hard, but arguably the most important element to get right when building a company.

In the real world, finding a co-founder comes with high transaction costs - searching, bargaining, evaluating. The Bridge introduces norms that reduce these transaction costs to give you an unfair advantage: a curated room of potential people to work with, alongside structure and frameworks to set standards and judge progress.

But The Bridge is an unusual environment, even for seasoned founders - if you've built a company before it's unlikely to have been with a stranger, in a house full of other strangers, on an 8-week timeframe. This document outlines how you should think about finding a long-term teammate in this context.

Important things to note:

  • The right cofounder is far more important than any one idea. Don't become so attached to a single idea that you lose the opportunity to work with an exceptional individual on something exciting.
  • The Bridge frameworks and environment have been designed to amplify your chances of finding the right cofounder. See them as tools for focus and prioritisation, rather than restrictions on what you can build.
  • Making fast, high quality decisions with limited information is the key - you are never going to have perfect information on every founder in the cohort, and you're setting yourself up for failure if you attempt to. Your team selection doesn't need to be perfect first time - this is the beauty of The Bridge environment. The threshold for breaking a team is low - if it's not working for any reason, discuss and if the issue is unsolvable issue break the team and move on.
  • Every second you are in an unproductive team without a clear edge, mission, or insight you are wasting time that could be spent building a company with someone else.
  • Don't play the game of "if I give the impression of being in a strong team then maybe I'll get investment in Week 9, and we can figure it out later". The prize of this pie-eating contest is more pie! All you're doing is locking yourself into that team for good - the only person you're cheating is yourself. Everything is opportunity cost.

The guidance below is just that. There are many ways to build a team, and if these frameworks don't work for your context we can help you think through options.

There are no rules.

What to look for in a co-founder

Complementary skillsets. People are naturally drawn to others that are similar to themselves. Those that have studied the same subject, like the same things, have built the same products. There's plenty to talk about and the conversation feels natural. Surely this is what you've been looking for! Unfortunately not. You're likely to be wrong if you look for social cues, instead of looking at who you can be most productive with. You don't want to duplicate yourself, but instead build with someone that brings something to the table that you cannot. In this way you both come up with ideas that wouldn't have been possible without your discrete knowledge, and move faster than would have been possible with a carbon copy of yourself.

Ease of ideation. Teambuilding & ideation are symbiotic, they can't be separated. The same idea with a different co-founder will become a completely new idea. Your ability to bounce ideas around and explore new avenues with someone is indicative of co-founder potential - if you build a company together you'll constantly need to creatively think your way out of problems together.

Deep mutual respect. The best co-founding teams feel equally lucky to be working with the other. Each person feels like their cofounder is out of their league. They can't believe they landed the best person in the cohort. You're looking for someone for whom you have a deep respect for - their skills, knowledge, ability, character - and who you trust to execute on their part of the business.

Shared values. Everyone wants to build a company for a different reason. Making sure you're aligned with your co-founder on the type of organization you want to create and why is an important foundation to set.

Until you have worked closely with someone you have little data on what they might be like as a potential co-founder. If you feel like you have an interesting starting point, some testable hypotheses, a good skills overlap and a good read on their values: jump into a team.

What does great feel like?

  • Good co-founding relationships have a multiplier effect. It's not just that you get on and work well together, but you can achieve orders of magnitude more than the two of you working separately could. You make more concrete progress together than apart. You're lifted by being a part of the team. You can feel it quickly; often within 24-48 hours. If you don't feel excited, driven and grateful within 2 days, it's likely not the right team.
  • Productivity is traction for teams. If you're productive together (learning rapidly, validating and invalidating hypotheses), this is signal on team quality.
  • The best teams just move faster. Each time they check in with an advisor they're asking different questions - either they've answered all their previous ones or have developed the idea to the point that those questions are no longer relevant. Speed is the key differentiator between teams that win and those that lose.

If you find yourself looking around the room and being jealous of other teams, you're probably in the wrong team yourself.

Common traps

  • Avoid pitching your ideas immediately to potential co-founders. Lead with your skills and experience to keep the surface area of possible things to work on as broad as possible. You would be surprised how many people end up working on something they never expected purely because they kept an open mind.
  • Falling in love with your cofounder. Not literally… You may find that you've developed a strong relationship with a co-founder but haven't managed to hit on any exciting starting points or ideas. You keep 'pivoting' but really are jumping from undifferentiated idea to undifferentiated idea in an effort to keep the team together. This is often paired with the unshakeable view that there isn't anyone better for you in the cohort; you vibe well and have met each other's partners - why not keep working together? The tough reality is that you cannot separate teambuilding and ideation - if you're not making constant linear ideation progress with a co-founder, it's likely not the right pair.
  • Chasing a "spark". It's rare that lightning strikes and you know immediately when you've found the right co-founder. That feeling of the 'right' match can develop over a prolonged period of high intensity work together. Rapidly cycling through ideation sessions with many different people until you feel a 'spark' runs the risk of missing relationships with the potential to grow into strong teams.
  • Letting productivity mask underlying problems. When you're making great progress on an idea it can be extremely tempting to ignore underlying issues in the team to avoid rocking the boat. This is a mistake - team issues always resurface later, and the longer you leave them the more catastrophic they will be. Have a difficult conversation, try and address the issues, and move on from the team if it's not resolvable.
  • Not spending time getting to know your cofounder on a personal level. If you get this relationship right, you'll be on a 10+ year journey together. You should spend time getting to know who your co-founder is and what makes them tick. Just because you're not looking for a friend doesn't mean they can't become one!

The Bridge teambuilding norms

There are many ways to build companies, but from our 15 years of experience constructing them in this environment, we recommend the following:

  • Teams of two. Selecting one co-founder is hard. Selecting two is even harder. Adding a third co-founder into the dynamic triples the number of relationships that need to be managed within the team. Often two co-founders end up wanting to go in one ideation direction, while the remaining founder tries to go in another.

    It's very rare (but not out of the question) that you actually need the skills of a 3rd co-founder in the team from Day 1 - it's also the most expensive decision you will ever make. Ask yourself - is this extra person worth parting with 1/3 of my company? Can we reach seed without them and hire in any missing skills?

  • 100% commitment to the team you're in. It's impossible to truly test a team unless you're all-in. Avoid working with multiple people for prolonged period of times simultaneously; you won't get useful signal. Pick a team, set a timeframe, and test with 100% focus.
  • Co-founders should work in the same place - try not to separate in the early days. It can be tempting to divide and conquer to cover more ground separately, but remember you're not just testing your idea, you're also testing the team. By all means run different streams of work, but try and be physically together for as much of the early months as possible.
  • We celebrate breakups. Time to breakup matters. One of the most counter-intuitive elements to The Bridge is that team breakups aren't something to be disappointed by, they're celebrated. Ultimately everyone in the cohort is here for the same thing, to figure out who to work with and on what - if a match isn't the right fit, for any reason, then it's in everyone's interest to address the issue and free each other back up. A classic error is to hold onto a team for weeks longer than you should have in the hope things will improve on their own - don't be afraid to move quickly.
  • 50:50 Share Split. Everyone is here to share in the creation of a business, both in the idea and also in equity. The vast majority of the company's lifetime is ahead of you, so even if you have entered The Bridge with a clear idea and some early progress it rarely makes sense to explore an uneven founder share split. There are exceptions, but we suggest this as a default.

Assessing your team on an ongoing basis - having hard conversations

From the beginning it's crucial to get in the habit of constantly assessing how happy and productive you are in your team.

It's important to remember that even if the intensity of your relationships means you quickly feel close to the person you're working with, you are lacking essential context that close family and friends will have about each of you; context built on years of understanding you and the events that happen around you. Never underestimate the ability of people to surprise you!

It's easy to become complacent once you feel like you've hit on the idea and forget to keep working on the team relationship. But your team is the most important asset you have. It requires constant work and proactive monitoring.

Why do teams break up?

We have created a norm at The Bridge where it is encouraged, even required, that you address the points below in some of the earliest conversations with your cofounder. Many of these might seem immediately uncomfortable, personal and private - co-founding with anyone, let alone a stranger, requires full honesty and transparency from the earliest possible moment of working together.

Roles & responsibilities

What will each of you eventually spend your time on?

One of the biggest reasons for breakdowns in co-founder relationships is misalignment on what each founder will eventually spend their time doing in the future. Do you your skillsets overlap too much? Do you both want to be doing the same activities? For some teams it is really clear from the outset who will be doing what. For others, especially those teams where there are overlapping skill sets, if this hasn't been discussed / is loosely defined it can create future problems.

We recommend drawing out a venn diagram with your co-founder and writing down which areas you think your abilities are most naturally drawn to, where you each excel. Ideally, it'll be complementary and balanced, knowing very well that this will change over time and that some skills and abilities you'll eventually need to hire for. You should have enough between you both as a core in order to move fast and be productive.

If however you are complementary but don't have enough core abilities to be productive, you need to have a hard conversation with your cofounder and talk it through.

Too much overlap will stall progress and cause friction and tension. This can be the most insidious setup if not addressed up-front; the most likely to cause a late-stage team break-up when there is more on the line than a few days work.

Personalities, incentives

You're all here because you want to build a generational company. Why is this important to you, and why is it important to your cofounder? This will differ for everyone. Is it a sense of self? Is it ego based? Is it monetary? Is it to change a world? To solve just that one problem?

Do you know what makes your co-founder tick? What makes your cofounder happy? How they deal with stressful situations? Resolve conflict? Have you met each others' partners or families? If your customers end up in a different geography, would you move? What is your financial runway?

Personality clashes can happen in teams that look the strongest. Productivity is high, so rather than rocking the boat with a tough conversation about a personality clash, the co-founders let it go unspoken.

It's often hard for anyone outside the team to detect this to detect this, but you can. Act on it.

Working styles, culture, values

You can be deliberate about team culture. This doesn't mean writing a mission statement and culture document on Day 1. But you have an opportunity to develop positive team norms and culture from the beginning. It is never too early to have these conversations and get a deeper understanding of how the other wants to work.

A good probing question is to ask what type of culture your cofounder wants to create? "It would make you proud to hear people describe your company's culture as…."?

Do you understand how your cofounder likes to work? There are no right or wrong answers. You might even have opposing answers and that's okay - see this as a data gathering exercise to give you the strongest possible foundation for moving forward:

  • What is your preferred method of communication?
  • How do you measure productivity?

Monitoring team health

Just as you are evaluating product and commercial progress in your team, you must be evaluating team health - especially important when you are getting to know each other.

Feedback is the most powerful tool to keep your team healthy - critical for 2 reasons:

  • The obvious: if something is annoying you, aggravating you, giving feedback allows you to fix that problem before it becomes a big issue.
  • The less obvious: in a large organisation, there are mechanisms to support your professional development. Performance reviews, peer appraisals, 360 degree feedback. In a two-person team, none of those exist - you are each other's boss, peer and direct reports all rolled into one. You are everything to your co-founder. You need to be 100% committed to their development. As a co-founder, you have an obligation to help them be the best possible leader within the company that they can be. And the way that you do that is by giving feedback. You are the only people who know what the other is great, good and bad at.

Issues won't 'sort themselves out' down the line (especially as pressure increases). They only get bigger and harder to fix and face once more is on the line, you've raised money, hired a team, etc.

Few prioritise this, but learning how to give (and receive) feedback to your co-founder often and effectively will change the trajectory of your team. It helps you focus on building a globally important company rather than just jumping through the next hoop.

Feedback is a gift, and a concrete skill you can develop.

Principles for feedback

You are providing guidance, which involves a mix of praise as well as criticism

Practice Radical Candor. Say what you really think while also caring about the person you're saying it to. Challenging people directly is often the best way to show you care. Helpful reading here.

Regularity of feedback matters. A recurring 'space' to air concerns/challenges can force you to work through things that you might not have done otherwise, meaning small issues don't snowball into bigger challenges. Regular feedback also mitigates 'heat of the moment' tensions. Put this in the calendar every day or two to begin with, then every week as the team progresses.

Quality of feedback matters. It's important to remember there will be a gap between intention and effect in your communication. You intend one thing, but the effect can be something totally different.

This gap exists even between people that know each other very well, so is particularly exacerbated among strangers.

What you mean to say is within your control, but how it comes across / is interpreted is not. This gap often leads to the biggest breakdowns in communication and trust between co-founders. It's always best to over clarify what you mean. A helpful heuristic: don't believe everything you think!

Feedback models

To make sure feedback remains objective (and not a personal attack) it can sometimes be helpful to follow a defined structure. There are many models but here are a few we have found helpful

  • OEPS - Observe > Effect > Pause > Suggest
    • "I observe…"
    • "It had this effect…"
    • "I suggest…"
  • SKS - Stop > Keep > Start
  • Stanford - I like > I wish > What if

If you find this tough, try the following questions...

  • When I say 'team dynamic', what's the first thing that comes into your head? Even someone's body language in response to this can be useful!
  • On a scale of 1-10, how happy do you currently feel in our team?
    • This is useful because it gives a numerical score that you can track over time. It may seem counterintuitive to put emotions/feelings into numbers but it can often be a less awkward way to trigger / begin a conversation. This is a very powerful tool.
  • What do you think your cofounder has found most difficult this week?
    • Answer this in front of each other. It can be super effective as it's less confrontational than saying "what have I done this week that's shit?". It forces you to be self-reflective and can lead to incredibly valuable / productive conversations.

The goal is to get to a place where you and your co-founder have built a code of signals between each other - you understand how the other one communicates, gives feedback and likes to receive it back. Close that gap between intention and effect.
